## About SOMIS

**SOMIS** (Student Organization of Management Information Systems) is a student-led professional organization at the University of Houston – Downtown dedicated to fostering the professional growth of MIS students. Through corporate partnerships, workshops, networking events, and community service, SOMIS bridges the gap between academic learning and real-world industry experience — aligned with the Marilyn Davies College of Business mission.

**Founded:** Fall 2024  
**Status:** Startup / Launch Semester  
**Affiliation:** UHD Marilyn Davies College of Business  
**Platforms:** Instagram · LinkedIn · Campus Groups

### 🔴 The Challenge — *What problem did we have to solve?*

Launching a student organization from scratch is one of the highest-difficulty marketing problems in a university environment. We had no existing audience, no brand recognition, no budget pipeline, and no proof of concept to leverage with corporate partners. Every challenge a startup faces — but with a student team and a single semester to prove ourselves.

The specific goals we set:

- **Build brand awareness from zero** across two professional platforms (Instagram & LinkedIn)
- **Drive consistent event attendance** across general meetings, corporate visits, workshops, and socials
- **Attract and retain members** in a competitive student org landscape at UHD
- **Establish corporate credibility** fast enough to land meaningful partnerships in Semester 1
- **Define SOMIS's visual identity and voice** — the brand that would represent MIS students at UHD for years to come

There was no playbook. We wrote it.

---

### 🔵 The Strategy — *How did we approach it?*

The core strategic insight: **in a startup org, marketing IS the organization's reputation.** Before we had programming, we had to make people believe something worth joining was being built.

**1. Brand Identity First**

Before the first post went live, my team and I defined SOMIS's brand pillars — *technical, professional, modern, approachable* — with a semester theme of **"Silicon Valley Business Techies."** Every visual, every caption, every flyer had to speak to that identity. We used Canva and Adobe Creative Cloud to develop a cohesive visual system: color palette, typography, logo usage guidelines, and tone of voice.

**2. Multi-Channel Content Architecture**

Rather than treating Instagram and LinkedIn as the same platform, we deployed channel-specific strategies:
- **Instagram** → community-building: event flyers, reels, recaps, memes, countdowns, interactive stories
- **LinkedIn** → professional credibility: corporate visit recaps, leadership spotlights, org milestones
- **Campus Groups** → member management, event RSVPs, official communications

**3. Campaign-Driven Event Promotion**

Every event got its own campaign — not just one flyer. We created teaser content, event-specific flyers, day-of reminders, and post-event recap content. Each campaign was treated as a storytelling arc with a beginning, middle, and end.

**4. Notion-Powered Team Operations**

I implemented **Notion** as our marketing team's operating system — project summaries, content calendars, task assignments, deadlines, and shared asset libraries. This kept a team of 3 aligned and executing across weekly deliverables while balancing coursework.

**5. Cross-Team Communication**

Used **GroupMe** for rapid coordination with other SOMIS leadership teams and **Microsoft Teams** for structured meetings and online collaboration — keeping the marketing team embedded in the larger org strategy, not siloed.

#### 🎬 The SOMIS Brand Reel

**The strategic problem:** A brand-new organization with no visual identity, no social proof, and no campus presence needs to earn attention fast. Text posts and static flyers alone don't create momentum — video does.

**The process:** We conceived, shot, and edited a brand reel from scratch to serve as SOMIS's first impression on both Instagram and LinkedIn. The reel was intentionally built to do three things simultaneously: establish the visual identity (color, motion, energy), communicate the org's professional-yet-youthful tone, and drive profile follows by giving people something worth sharing. Every frame was planned — this was not a casual recording, it was a directed creative production executed by a student team with no production budget, using available tools and resourcefulness.

**Why it mattered:** In a competitive student org landscape, the organizations that look like they have their act together attract the members, the partners, and the attention. The reel signaled from day one that SOMIS operated at a different level.

#### 🖥️ Interactive Presentations

**The strategic problem:** General meetings are where organizations win or lose members. If your first meeting is a wall of text on a plain slide deck, people don't come back. SOMIS needed presentations that felt like an experience — something a student would talk about after they left the room.

**The process:** I led the design of our general meeting presentations with the audience firmly in mind — college students aged 18–25 who consume content on TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube. That meant animated transitions, themed visuals, embedded motion graphics, pop culture references baked into layouts, and slide designs that matched our semester theme. The Marketing Responsibilities slide (Explore / Create / Brand) was built to make our team's scope feel exciting and creative, not like a chore list. The 2025 Goals slide used motion and sticky-note metaphors to frame our roadmap in terms students could immediately connect with — Dream Big, Set Goals, Take Action.

**Why it mattered:** These presentations drove engagement, membership retention, and word-of-mouth. Students who attended told others. The interactive format also demonstrated SOMIS's technical capabilities to both members and prospective corporate partners who sat in on sessions.

#### 👥 Leadership & Branding — The Silicon Valley Theme

**The strategic problem:** A founding officer team needs to project authority and cohesion before the organization has earned it through track record. The brand has to do the work that history hasn't had time to build yet.

**The process:** As Director of Marketing, I conceptualized and directed the founding officer photoshoot as a deliberate brand-building exercise. The semester theme — *Silicon Valley Business Techies* — needed to be visible and felt, not just described. We chose a striking architectural location, styled around contrast (dark vs. light, structured vs. relaxed), and executed a shoot that positioned the team as confident, unified, and forward-looking. Every detail — the poses, the styling, the backdrop — was chosen to reinforce a brand identity that said: *this organization means business.*

**Why it mattered:** This photo became the face of SOMIS leadership across all platforms. It set the tone for how the org carried itself publicly and gave prospective members and corporate partners a visual anchor for who was running things.

#### 🚀 Launch Campaign — First General Meeting

**The strategic problem:** Your first general meeting is your most important marketing moment of the semester. It sets the attendance baseline, the energy standard, and the word-of-mouth trajectory for everything that follows. With zero existing audience, we had to generate curiosity and urgency entirely from scratch.

**The process:** The First General Meeting flyer was designed to stand out in a campus environment saturated with generic event posters. We leaned into the tech aesthetic — dark background, glowing network globe, cinematic typography — to signal that this wasn't a typical student org. The flyer was distributed digitally across Campus Groups, Instagram stories, and direct outreach, with a QR code for easy Zoom access to lower the barrier to attendance. The campaign treated the meeting itself as a product launch — not just an informational session.

**Why it mattered:** The turnout at the first general meeting validated the entire marketing approach and gave SOMIS the social proof it needed to build from. A packed room in Semester 1 became the foundation for everything else — membership growth, corporate interest, and the eventual Student Org of the Year nomination.

#### 🎨 Campaign Flyers — Designed for the Audience

**The strategic problem:** Campus flyers are invisible. Students scroll past dozens of generic event announcements every week. To drive attendance and engagement, every SOMIS event needed a campaign identity — not just a date and a time on a colored background.

**The process:** Each flyer was designed as a standalone creative piece with its own visual language, tailored to the specific event and audience moment. The approach was audience-first throughout:

- **2nd General Meeting** — gamified with a retro Mario-style design to signal that SOMIS meetings are fun, not just professional. An intentional contrast to the serious first-meeting flyer, keeping momentum playful and building a personality for the org.
- **Ansys Company Connections** — bold and technical, engineered to feel like a corporate product launch. The design matched the energy of the brand being featured, making the event feel prestigious and worth showing up for.
- **AI Workshop** — futuristic and clean, built to appeal to tech-curious students and position SOMIS at the forefront of emerging technology conversations on campus.
- **Guest Speaker / Tech Career Roadmap** — centered the speaker as the draw, using portrait photography and high-contrast layouts that mirror professional conference materials. Gave students a reason to care about *who* was coming, not just *that* someone was coming.
- **November Calendar** — a monthly events flyer that reduced member drop-off by giving the audience a complete picture of what was coming in one post. Presented in a warm seasonal aesthetic to feel timely and human, not transactional.
- **Volunteering Event** — community-forward design that balanced the org's professional image with genuine civic engagement at NRG Stadium, reinforcing that SOMIS was about more than networking.
- **SOMIS Stole** — a merchandising campaign targeting graduating members, combining urgency (payment deadline) with pride (the SOMIS brand on graduation regalia). This flyer doubled as a retention tool — members who purchased stoles became walking brand ambassadors at commencement.

**Why it mattered:** Consistent, high-quality flyer campaigns across the semester trained the campus audience to expect quality from SOMIS content and to pay attention when a new post dropped. It built the visual brand recognition that made corporate partners take the org seriously from the outside.

#### 🏢 Corporate Visits & Partnership Events

**The strategic problem:** Corporate partnerships don't fall into a student org's lap — especially not in the first semester of existence. Companies need to believe an organization has an audience worth reaching, a team worth investing in, and a brand that reflects well on them. We had to manufacture that credibility before we technically had the track record to back it up.

**The process:** The marketing team played an active role in both securing and amplifying every corporate relationship. On the front end, our social media presence, professional flyer campaigns, and branded content gave us the appearance of an established org — which opened doors. On the back end, every corporate visit was treated as a full content production: I photographed the events, the team created recap content for both Instagram and LinkedIn, and we made sure every partner saw their brand reflected positively in our coverage. That feedback loop — visit, document, publish, amplify — made partners feel valued and created a public record of credibility that attracted the next partner.

**Microsoft, Dell, Protiviti, KBR, and Ansys** were all secured and visited within a single semester. Each visit was documented, published, and used to raise the bar for what came next.

#### 📱 Social Media Presence — Built from Zero

**The strategic problem:** Social media is the public scorecard for a student organization. Follower count signals legitimacy. Content quality signals capability. Consistency signals that the org is real, active, and worth following. We started at zero on both platforms — no followers, no posts, no history.

**The process:** We built Instagram and LinkedIn simultaneously with distinct strategies for each. Instagram was our community channel — high-frequency posting, visual storytelling, reels, event countdowns, and recaps that kept the audience engaged between events. LinkedIn was our professional channel — milestone posts, corporate visit recaps, and leadership content that spoke directly to the org's credibility with industry recruiters and partners. As all-platform overseer, I ensured that content was consistent in voice and visual identity across every post, while the Social Media Analyst tracked engagement metrics to inform what was resonating and what needed adjusting week over week.

**Why it mattered:** Reaching 210+ Instagram followers and 200+ LinkedIn followers in a single semester — starting from zero — is a measurable proof of concept for the marketing strategy as a whole. These weren't vanity numbers; they represented real students, real professionals, and real corporate contacts who chose to stay connected to what SOMIS was building. The platforms now continue to grow beyond the founding team's tenure, which is the real mark of a sustainable marketing system.
